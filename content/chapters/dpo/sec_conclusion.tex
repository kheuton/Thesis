We hope this work enables future researchers designing decision-aware learning techniques and practitioners implementing them to better understand and evaluate the strengths of different models.
In particular, we have provided evidence that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Many tasks commonly used to benchmark decision-aware methods are too simple or too well-specified to benefit from decision aware learning. Two-stage, decision-blind approaches should be attempted and given sufficient hyperparameter tuning budget. As for particular tasks: the Budget Allocation task appears to be a good candidate for comparing decision-aware learning methods, but the best methods currently achieve low ($<5\%$) regret. The Bipartite Matching task could possibly be a revealing task, as the best methods in this task still achieve roughly $90\%$ regret. Finally, the Portfolio task is not combinatorial optimization. We caution against its use without sufficiently noting this and separating the interpretation of results on this task from other decision aware tasks.
    \item Through analysis of the synthetic data benchmark of \citeauthor{huang2024decisionfocusedPG}, we have shown two important dimensions with which to analyze decision-aware methods: ``What solution does this method prefer?" and ``What solution is this method capable of learning''. We have also demonstrated how varying key hyperparameters can change the answer to both of these questions.
    \item Finally, we extend this analysis to a real-world example of predicting locations for public health and wildlife interventions. We attempt to explain why different decision-aware methods have reached markedly different decisions, and we see that DPO prefers a solution with lower decision regret in this case.
\end{itemize}
