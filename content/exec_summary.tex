Better decisions in public health with limited budgets

\begin{itemize}
    \item Prediction error is the wrong goal
    
    \item It's difficult to train models on decision error

    \item The methods for training decision error are opaque and difficult to use.
\end{itemize}

The existing solutions to these problems in the modern analysis of EHRs are inadequate. Existing deep models handle feature missingness by either using overly simplistic imputation strategies \citep{purushotham2018benchmarking, harutyunyan2019multitask} or by imputing deterministically without uncertainty \citep{che2018recurrent, cao2018brits}. Moreover, these models often neglect data-dependent  missingness patterns (for e.g. certain lab measurements are only collected for critically ill patients), are sensitive to the initialization of missing values \citep{cao2018brits}, or require hundreds of thousands of parameters with a lack of clinical interpretability \citep{cao2018brits, purushotham2018benchmarking, harutyunyan2019multitask}. Later, when these models are deployed, they often lead up to 90$\%$ of all alarms being false alarms \citep{fernandes2020detecting}. Another common issue is that ML models rely heavily on labels and obtaining precise annotations of disease labels from clinicians is often laborious and expensive. 

The goal of my thesis is to address these 3 barriers to deploying ML prediction systems in clinical care settings. I will introduce new probabilistic models to handle missing data, limited labels and new paradigms for training to address false alarms and unlabeled data.
